# Facilitation Notes: ``Vignette 4 - Urgent Recruitment Poster''

## Overview
This vignette simulates a common grassroots volunteer scenario: receiving an urgent, detailed request from a peer to create a recruitment poster with minimal time for discussion. The exercise demonstrates how SAFER can be applied even under time pressure, with emphasis on rapid risk assessment and the Reject principle as a legitimate protective choice.

---

## Scenario Setup

**Time available**: 20-25 minutes total

**The scenario**:

You receive this WhatsApp message from a fellow volunteer:

> *"Hi - need you to draft a recruitment poster ASAP for new volunteers. We're launching a virtual English tutoring program for teens in Gaza starting next month. Need: program description, volunteer requirements (B2 English minimum, 3hrs/week commitment), application link, and a simple graphic. **URGENT** - we're behind schedule. Can you get this done today? Attached are notes from our planning call - I'm in a meeting for the next 3 hours so can't discuss further. Please just make it happen. Thanks!"*

**Attached notes include**:
- Program serves 50 teens (ages 14-17)
- Locations: Khan Younis, Rafah
- 12-week program, starts August 1
- Volunteers must attend 3 training sessions (dates included)
- A photo of a previous workshop (containing visible participant faces)
- A list of local partner schools and contact names
- Logo of a small local NGO (non-sensitive)
- Notes on cultural considerations: "avoid anything that feels like charity/western saviorism"

---

## Facilitation Guide

### Phase 1: Scenario Introduction (3 minutes)

**Facilitator says**:

> "You're a volunteer coordinator. Another volunteer just messaged you with an urgent request to create a recruitment poster. There's no room for discussion - they need it done today and they're unavailable for follow-up. We're going to walk through how SAFER would help you make this decision, even under time pressure."

**Key context to emphasize**:
- This is a real scenario that volunteers face regularly
- The request comes from a peer, not a supervisor - no institutional authority
- The urgency is real (program launch deadline)
- You have no budget to hire a designer
- You have no way to consult cultural experts or community members

**Prompt for participants**:

> "Before we dive in, what's your gut reaction to this request? What feels important? What worries you?"

---

### Phase 2: Rapid SAFER Walkthrough (15 minutes)

**Facilitator note**: This should be a facilitated discussion, not a lecture. Use the following as a guide for your facilitation.

---

#### S - Set Purpose (2 minutes)

**Facilitator asks participants**:

> "If you were to accept this task, what would you specify as the purpose? What are the boundaries?"

**Expected discussion points**:

- **Purpose**: Create a one-page recruitment poster for volunteers
- **DO**: Use attached notes for program description, requirements, key dates
- **DON'T**:
  - Do not use identifiable photos of learners
  - Do not include specific location names beyond what's necessary
  - Do not copy-paste partner contact names
  - Do not generate images that could be misconstrued (e.g., western-style classroom scenes)
  - Do not use this for anything beyond recruitment outreach

**Critical question**:

> "Is this task actually within our purpose? We're a humanitarian group, not a marketing agency. Does creating a polished recruitment poster serve our mission, or does it risk looking like something we're not?"

**Facilitator note**: This question helps participants recognize that purpose-setting isn't just about defining the task - it's about deciding whether the task is appropriate at all.

---

#### A - Assess Harms (3 minutes)

**Facilitator asks participants**:

> "Let's run through the Assess Harms template. For each category, what's the most likely harm?"

**Use this prompt template** (modified for time):

*Task: Creating a recruitment poster for volunteers using notes and previous workshop photos.*

*Audience: Prospective volunteers (public-facing on social media, potentially shared beyond intended audience)*

**Categories to discuss**:

| Category | Discussion Questions |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Misinformation** | Could the poster overpromise what volunteers will do? Could specific dates or requirements be incorrect? Could it misrepresent what it's like to volunteer? |
| **Privacy** | The attached photo shows learner faces. Should we use it? What about partner school names? Contact people? Program location? |
| **Who could be harmed** | If the poster is tone-deaf, who suffers? The learners? The organization's credibility? The volunteer applicants who show up and find something different? |
| **Cultural fit** | What assumptions does an AI tool make about what 'recruitment' looks like? What about ideas of 'volunteering' or 'helping'? What images does it generate? |

**Facilitator note**: The attached photo of learners' faces is a **red flag**. This is the kind of detail that's easy to miss in a rush. Emphasize that even if the volunteer didn't ask for the photo to be included, the fact that it's attached creates a temptation to use it.

**Time-saving tip**: If participants are struggling, offer the Claude/DeepSeek/Thaura assessment from the paper (Section 4.3) as an example of how models themselves can help identify risks.

---

#### F - Fortify (3 minutes)

**Facilitator asks participants**:

> "We can't get cultural validation. We can't consult the learners. We can't run this by the volunteer who messaged us. What fortification *can* we do?"

**Expected discussion**:

Given the constraints, **Level 2-3 fortification**:

1. **Cross-model verification**: Generate the poster with one model, critique with another
2. **Internal peer review**: Find another volunteer (anyone available) to review the draft quickly
3. **Apply the fortification prompt**:

> *"Review this recruitment poster for use in a humanitarian education context. Identify: (1) Any assumptions about normal life that don't apply to Gaza, (2) Any imagery that could be stereotypical or tone-deaf, (3) Any language that feels 'savior complex' or colonial, (4) Any details that could identify specific learners, (5) Any cultural mismatches. Be specific and practical."*

**Critical recognition**:

> "Even if we do all this, the highest fortification level we can reach is Level 3. We cannot get community review. So the question becomes: **is that acceptable for a public-facing poster?** "

**Facilitator note**: This is the crux. Participants need to recognize that even with best efforts, they will be creating an output that cannot be properly validated. The framework must make this visible rather than pretending it's okay.

---

#### E - Explain Use (2 minutes)

**Facilitator asks participants**:

> "Assume we proceed. What disclosure would be appropriate?"

**Discussion should cover**:

- **Label**: "AI-assisted recruitment poster. All content reviewed by volunteers, but not validated by community members."
- **Feedback loop**: "Contact [email] with concerns. We welcome corrections."
- **Limitations**: Acknowledge that the poster represents volunteer efforts, not community voice

**Critical question**:

> "Does transparent disclosure mitigate the risks, or does it simply make us feel better while still doing something potentially harmful?"

---

#### R - Reject (5 minutes)

**Facilitator says**:

> "This is the moment. We've been walking through SAFER, but remember: the Reject principle can be invoked at any time. Not just at the end."

**Present these options**:

**Option A: Full Rejection**

> "We decide not to use an LM for this task at all. We either do it manually (even if it's basic and takes longer) or we message the volunteer back and say: 'I can't do this justice without some discussion. Can we connect briefly even for 5 minutes?'"

**Option B: Partial Rejection (Re-scoping)**

> "We use LMs for internal drafting only. We generate text ideas, but the final poster is manually created using those ideas as inspiration. We do NOT use AI for image generation. We explicitly tell the volunteer: 'Here's what I drafted with AI assistance, but I can't finalize without your input.'"

**Option C: Proceed with Fortification**

> "We proceed but at Level 3 (cross-model + internal review), we document limitations clearly, and we add strong disclosure. We accept the risk because the program is urgent and we believe the benefits outweigh the harms."

**Facilitator asks participants**:

> "Which option would you choose, and why? What would you actually do in this scenario?"

**Expected discussion points**:

- **For Rejection**: "The risks are too high for a public-facing artifact. We can't properly validate this, so we shouldn't put it out there."
- **For Re-scoping**: "We can use AI to think, but we don't use AI to speak for us. The poster has to come from human volunteers."
- **For Proceeding**: "The program needs volunteers. The alternative is no program, which is worse than a potentially imperfect poster. But we need to be honest about the limitations."

**Facilitator note**: There's no right answer. The point is to demonstrate that Reject is a legitimate choice, not a failure. Some participants will choose to proceed; others will choose to reject. Both are valid SAFER outcomes.

**Critical observation to highlight**:

> "Notice that the urgency and lack of discussion are *themselves* red flags. The conditions that make this task urgent are the same conditions that make it risky. The volunteer who messaged you is unavailable to discuss, which means you can't clarify boundaries. The deadline is tight, which pushes you toward shortcuts. These are exactly the conditions where careful reasoning is most needed and hardest to maintain."

---

### Phase 3: Debrief and Reflection (5 minutes)

**Facilitator asks participants**:

**Reflection questions**:

1. "What was the hardest part of applying SAFER under time pressure?"

2. "Did the framework help you see something you would have missed otherwise?"

3. "If you chose to proceed, what would you need to feel comfortable? If you chose to reject or re-scope, what convinced you?"

4. "What's the one thing from this exercise you'll remember next time you're in this situation?"

**Facilitator note**: Encourage participants to identify the specific SAFER principle or question that was most valuable to them. Some will value the harm assessment; others will appreciate having permission to reject.

**Wrap-up statement**:

> "The purpose of this exercise isn't to tell you what to do. It's to show you that even under pressure, you have choices. You can pause, you can ask questions, you can say no. The structure of SAFER makes those choices explicit and legitimate. You're not failing by rejecting an LM; you're making a responsible decision."

---

## Facilitator Notes on Key Themes

### Theme 1: The Urgency Trap
Urgent requests are where mistakes happen. The framework is most needed when you feel most pressure to skip it. This vignette is designed to surface that tension.

### Theme 2: The Power of Silence
The volunteer is unavailable for discussion. This is a red flag. You can't proceed without clarity. SAFER's "Set Purpose" principle requires explicit boundaries, and you can't set them without discussion.

### Theme 3: The Photo as a Test
The attached workshop photo with visible learner faces is a test. Would participants notice it? Would they even consider using it? This is a concrete example of how easy it is to compromise privacy when you're in a hurry.

### Theme 4: Community Review as Aspirational
In many grassroots groups, community review will be aspirational, not achievable. The framework needs to be honest about this. Proceeding at Level 3 is a compromise; acknowledging it as such is better than pretending it's adequate.

## Variation: Individual Exercise

If participants are working alone or asynchronously:

1. **Read the scenario** (5 min)
2. **Apply SAFER individually** (10 min)
   - Write brief answers for each principle
   - Focus on the critical decision point: reject, re-scope, or proceed?
3. **Share in a group** (10 min)
   - Each person shares their decision and reasoning
   - Compare differences

---

## Variation: Extended Session

If you have more time (40-60 minutes):

1. **Create the poster** using the attached notes and an LM of your choice (10 min)
2. **Fortify the output** using another model (5 min)
3. **Peer review** (5 min)
4. **Revise** based on feedback (5 min)
5. **Discuss**: Does the process change your confidence in the output? (5 min)
6. **Compare** to a poster created without LMs (10 min)

## Common Participant Questions

### Q: "Isn't this too much for a simple poster?"

> A: "Is it? What happens if the poster accidentally reveals learner identities? What happens if it feels tone-deaf and the community is offended? What happens if volunteers show up and find something different from what was promised? Even a 'simple' poster has real consequences."

### Q: "But we need volunteers. Isn't doing something better than nothing?"

> A: "That's a real argument. But the question is: are we doing something *good*? Or are we doing something that risks being harmful? The Reject principle isn't about doing nothing; it's about doing something else. Maybe we post a simple text message instead of a polished poster. Maybe we wait until we can discuss it. Maybe we ask the volunteer to handle it themselves."

### Q: "We don't have time for all this SAFER stuff."

> A: "The exercise is designed to show you that SAFER doesn't have to take hours. Even 10 minutes of structured reflection can catch major risks. The poster in Vignette 3 could have avoided an embarrassing outcome with just a few minutes of harm assessment."

### Q: "What if the volunteer who messaged me gets angry if I say no?"

> A: "That's a valid concern. But the Reject principle is rooted in volunteer autonomy. You have the right to say no when you can't responsibly proceed. The framework makes that choice legitimate and defensible. You can say: 'I used SAFER and determined I can't responsibly do this without more input.'"

---

## Practical Takeaways for Participants

1. **Don't skip Set Purpose** - even a 30-second boundary check can prevent drift
2. **The Assess Harms prompt template** is your friend - copy and adapt it
3. **The photo of learners is a trap** - if you see identifiable faces, don't use them
4. **Cross-model verification** is available to everyone - use it
5. **Reject is not failure** - it's responsible decision-making
6. **Urgency is a red flag** - the conditions that create urgency are the conditions that require more care, not less

## Conclusion

This vignette demonstrates that SAFER can be applied even under time pressure, and that the Reject principle provides legitimate protection when risks remain unresolved. The urgency of a task does not override ethical responsibility. By making choices explicit and legitimate, SAFER helps volunteers make better decisions even when they're pressured, uncertain, or working alone.
